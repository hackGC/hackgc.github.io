What is hackGC?

hackGC is a weekend-long event where Grinnellians come together in teams to collaborate, innovate and build a piece of technology (Minimum Viable Product/Prototype) to help alleviate a social justice issue. Come brainstorm and implement solutions with us! No coding experience necessary!

As students at a small liberal arts college with an even smaller CS department, we're as aware as anybody else that there often seems to exist a "coder:non-coder" binary. Another purpose of hackGC, therefore, is to bridge that divide by offering a series of inspiring talks and workshops revolving around the intersection between social justice and technology, an excellent line-up of technical mentors, and an exhilarating hacking experience.

John Norman, '82'

VP of Technology and Security Officer at Iora Health

Recipe: Combine healthcare, technology, and a fresh approach to billing. Stir. What do you get?

The path to social justice for healthcare in the United States is made difficult because of the powerful interests and patterns surrounding it: insurance companies, awkward delivery of care, and bad technology. In this talk, John Norman will talk about how his colleagues have attempted to "bring humanity to healthcare" by focussing on and rebooting primary care. In his work, business and ethical choices affect all parts of the business including core engineering.

Biography

John G. Norman graduated from Grinnell College in 1982, where he majored in English. After graduating he came back to the college and worked for two years as a programmer/analyst. Norman then went to Harvard where he earned a PhD in English, focusing on the relationship between Renaissance literature and medicine. Starting in 1993, Norman taught at the Ohio State University. In his final year at Ohio State he returned to technology as the Director of the pioneering. Computers in Composition and Literature program.

Moving back to Cambridge, Mass. in 1999, Norman wrote articles and reviews about teaching with technology and taught online a course in “Digital Culture” at the New School in New York City. He started work as a software developer at Cambridge Incubator (now Cambridge Innovation Center) and its affiliate companies. In 2003 he co-founded the VC-backed h3.com, which provided for tracking and rewarding job referrals via email and the web. Over this period, he taught Java and Ruby on Rails at Harvard Extension, and contributed a chapter to the book Java Enterprise in a Nutshell (2005).

In 2010, with former colleagues from Cambridge Innovation Center, he was the technical co-founder of Roomzilla, a system for managing conference rooms that utilizes tablet computers as ambient indicators of reservation status. At present, Norman is the VP of Technology and Security Officer, whose broad mission is to change primary care as we know it (on the tech team, they say that they are changing primary care, one line of code at a time...). He also occasionally teaches engineering at the University of Minnesota.

David Neville

Ph.D, Digital Liberal Arts Specialist; Project Manager, Immersive Experiences Lab

VR for the Liberal Arts and Social Good

This hackGC presentation will examine the technological and psychological affordances that make virtual reality (VR) particularly well-suited for investigating liberal arts topics and promoting social justice issues. True VR allows a user to move and rotate in all three directions and interact with objects as a person would in the real world, which in turn can foster a sense of being physically present as an autonomous individual in recreated, remote, or even hypothetical sites. Not only can this sense of presence be useful for contextualizing knowledge within the spaces of these sites, but it can potentially also be used to transfer knowledge between the virtual environment and the real world.

Provided that hardware costs continue to fall and more VR experiences are released as open educational resources, VR also has the potential to democratize learning by giving a wider range of users access to sites that would be too difficult, dangerous, or expensive to see in person. Finally, by allowing users to to assume the first-person viewpoint of another human being and to experience reality from the perspective of this individual, VR has demonstrated the ability to promote empathy, self-other merging, and perspective taking.

Biography

David Neville earned a PhD in German language and literature, with an emphasis in medieval German studies and Latin, in 2002 from Washington University in St. Louis. Having developed an interest in educational technology, he enrolled in computer science and programming courses at Weber State University, eventually transferring to Utah State University to earn a MS in Instructional Technology and Learning Sciences. David is particularly interested in situated cognition theory and how goal-directed activity in 3D, VR, and AR environments can assist students in creating mental narratives that structure and guide the learning process.

Liz Rodrigues

Humanities and Digital Scholarship Librarian

Open Education: A Movement & A Community of Practice

The Open Education movement seeks to harness the potential of Web-based publication to lower the barriers of cost and access to education. This workshop will introduce you to the history, ethos, and practices of this movement. We will then work together to brainstorm how we might continue to open education at Grinnell College.

Erik Simpson

PhD, Professor of English

Humanities-Computer Interaction: Social Justice and the Digital Humanities

This talk will showcase some of the ways in which humanists are using digital methods to illuminate and influence social issues. Part of the talk will highlight a number of recent projects at Grinnell.

Biography

Erik Simpson is a scholar of British Romantic and Transatlantic literature, as well as the digital humanities—the field that lives at the intersection of computational methods and humanistic inquiry. He is Grinnell’s Principal Investigator of Digital Bridges for Humanistic Inquiry, a four-year grant from the Andrew W. Mellon foundation supporting collaboration between Grinnell and the University of Iowa in the digital liberal arts.

Samuel A. Rebelsky

PhD, Professor of Computer Science

Accessible Technology

Technology has the capability to make materials accessible to differently abled people, people with differences in hearing, vision, movement, and more. But technology also has the capability to disenfranchise the differently abled. In this hour, I'll talk about issues of accessibility and technology and then we'll discuss a case study relating to accessibility.
